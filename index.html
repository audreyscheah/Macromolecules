Macromolecules

Inroduction

Macromolecules, otherwise known as biomolecules, are large, organic polymers that consist of smaller units, called monomers.



Dehydration Synthesis and Hydrolysis

Monomers are chemically combined with each other through dehydration synthesis, which releases water, while polymers are broken down though hydrolysis, which occurs upon the addition of water to the substance.



Carbohydrates

Carbohydrates consist of carbon, hydrogen, and oxygen.

monomer: monosaccharides (simple sugars)

  • glucose (produced by plants through photosynthesis)
  • galactose (found in milk)
  • fructose (found in fruits)
  • ribose / deoxyribose (five carbon; found in nucleic acids)

disaccharides (monosaccharide + monosaccharide)

  • maltose (glucose + glucose; malt sugar)
  • lactose (glucose + galactose; milk sugar)
  • sucrose (glucose + fructose; table sugar)

polymer: polysaccharides (complex carbohydrates)

  • chitin (fungi cell walls; insect exoskeletons)
  • cellulose (plant cell walls; fiber)
  • glycogen (animal stored sugar)
  • starch (plant stored sugar; consists of amylose and amylopectin)

Carbohydrates provide short-term energy and contribute to cell structure.



Lipids

Lipids consist of carbon, hydrogen, and oxygen.

monomers:

  • glycerol
  • fatty acids
    • saturated (maximum bonding between carbon and hydrogebn; usually solid; originate from animals)
    • unsaturated (contain carbon double bond(s); usually liquid; originate from plants)
    • trans (unsaturated fat in a rigid, solid structure; usually artificial; allow for longer preservation)

polymers:

  • fats (solid, originate from animals)
  • oils (liquid, originate from plants)
  • waxes (solid originate from plants)
  • phospholipids (contain phosphorous; make up cell membranes)
  • steroids (ex. cholesterol)

Lipids provide energy storage, insulation, and protection.



Proteins

Proteins consist of carbon, hydrogen, oxygen, and nitrogen.

monomer: amino acids (peptides)

There are twenty amino acid types, nine of which are essential amino acids that cannot be synthesized by the human body.

can be either hydrophobic (ex. alenine, leucine, valine) or hydrophilic (ex. histodine, glutamine, serine)

polymer: polypeptide chains

primary structure: sequence of amino acids (peptide bonding)

secondary structure: alpha helix or beta pleated sheet (hydrogen bonding)

tertiary structure: 3-D polypeptide chain (disulfide, hydrogen, and ionic bonding; hydrophobic amino acids are located towars the inside while hydrophilic amino acids are located towards the outside)

quaternary structure: multiple polypeptide chains

types of proteins:

  • enzymes: catalyze chemical reactions (ex. amylase - breaks down amylose)
  • structural: support body shape and structure (ex. keratin - hair, horns, feathers; collagen - connective tissue)
  • transport: support circulation of substances throughout body (ex. hemoglobin - transports oxygen in blood; aquaporins - manages entrance and exit of water into and out of cells)
  • motor/contractile: enable motion (ex. myosin + actin - muscle contraction)
  • receptor: detect stimuli (ex. rhodopsin - in eye)
  • regulatory: maintain homeostasis (ex. insulin - regulates glucose levels / blood sugar)
  • defensive: protect body from pathogens (ex. interleukin 2, antibodies)

amino acid order determines protein shape

protein shape determines protein function

protein shape can be altered by genetic mutations, heat, or a change in pH



Nucleic Acids

Nucleic acids consist of carbon, hydrogen, oxygen, nitrogen, and phosphorous.

monomer: nucleotides (contain a phosphate group, a five carbon sugar, and a nitrogenous base - adenine, thymine, uracil, guanine, cytosine)

polymers:

  • ATP (adenosine triphosphate; nucleotide with three phosphate groups; form of energy)
  • RNA (ribonucleic acid; usually single stranded)
    • microRNA (binds to mRNA)
    • mRNA (messenger RNA; copies and transports genetic information)
    • rRNA (ribosomal RNA; found in ribosomes)
    • tRNA (transfer RNA; supports protein synthesis)
  • DNA (deoxyribonucleic acid; usually double stranded; provides genetic information)
